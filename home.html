Bio

My interests lie at the intersection of health policy and emergency care quality and value. I am primarily interested in leveraging diverse data sources and research methods to improve the value proposition of emergency care in the United States. My prior work has focused on the role of emergency departments in the broader healthcare system and the evolution of acute care over time. As of August 2022, I am in my first year in the National Clinician Scholars Program at the Institute for Healthcare Policy and Innovation as part Veterans Affairs-Ann Arbor/the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor, Michigan. I hold an appointment as a Visiting Research Scientist at Yale School of Medicine in the Department of Emergency Medicine, where I completed my residency training in emergency medicine, in New Haven, Connecticut. I am also a Fellow in Quality and Administration for the American College of Emergency Physicians, based in Dallas, Texas. I hold an M.D. from Wayne State University School of Medicine and an M.H.S. from Yale School of Medicine.

Consequences of Health System Strain

My most recent work has focused on health system strain, broadly defined and inclusive of bed capacity restraints as well as emergency department boarding and crowding, during COVID-19. In the Journal of Hospital Medicine, our team reported that geographic areas with fewer intensive care unit beds per COVID-19 case had greater COVID-19 death rates in the early pandemic period. Ongoing work in this space is now supported by a Society for Academic Emergency Medicine Foundation Emerging Infectious Disease and Disaster Preparedness grant mechanism with my academic mentor from Yale, Arjun Venkatesh MD MBA MHS (Principal Investigator).

Transportation Accessibility of COVID-19 Vaccination

When the first mRNA vaccines were distributed for COVID-19, I was asked by the Yale Medicine's Director of Population Health, Brita Roy MD MPH MHS, to generate reports on the distribution of existing vaccination clinics, and their accessibility by public transit. I pulled together a team with the requisite statistical and content-area expertise to provide the Yale team with useful insights. We plotted existing vaccination sites alongside ‘isochrones’ for 30-minute travel time by public transportation. This involved writing Python scripts to ‘scrap’ geospatial data on vaccination clinics, generating the ‘isochrones’ for 30-minute travel time in qGIS, and characterizing populations at the census tract level with data from the American Community Survey. While this did not ultimately result in an academic publication, this was one of my most inspired projects.

Health Literacy

My earliest academic projects were with Helen Levy PhD at the Institute for Social Research, where I worked for a short time as a research assistant. We used data from the Health and Retirement Study to explore how health literacy is associated with access to care and internet use in older adults. These were my first foray into methodological tools in health services research on of lasting importance for the U.S. healthcare system.
